Selecting, Implementing, and Communicating Inference Procedures

Understanding and correctly applying statistical inference procedures is a cornerstone of AP Statistics. This involves a systematic approach to choose the right method, execute it accurately, and clearly convey the findings in context.

1. Selecting the Appropriate Procedure

The first critical step is identifying which inferential procedure to use. This decision hinges on several key aspects of your study design and research question.

A. Identify the Research Question and Parameter of Interest

  • Estimation: Do you want to estimate a population parameter (e.g., proportion, mean, difference) with a confidence interval?
  • Hypothesis Testing: Do you want to test a claim about a population parameter (e.g., proportion, mean, difference, relationship, distribution) using a significance test?

B. Identify the Type of Data

  • Categorical Data: Proportions, counts.
  • Quantitative Data: Means, values that can be measured.

C. Identify the Number of Samples/Groups

  • One Sample: Inference about a single population parameter (e.g., $ p $ , $ \mu $ ).
  • Two Samples: Inference about the difference between two population parameters (e.g., $ p_1 - p_2 $ , $ \mu_1 - \mu_2 $ ). This could be independent samples or Paired Data.
  • Multiple Groups/Categories: Comparing more than two proportions or distributions (Setting Up a Chi-Square Goodness of Fit Test, Setting Up a Chi-Square Test for Homogeneity or Independence).
  • Relationship between Two Quantitative Variables: Inference about the slope of a regression line (Setting Up a Test for the Slope of a Regression Model).

Mastering statistical inference involves more than just calculating numbers; it requires a deep understanding of when and how to apply various procedures and how to effectively communicate the findings. This note page outlines the key steps in this process.

1. Selecting the Appropriate Inference Procedure

The choice of inference procedure is paramount. It depends on the specific research question, the type of data collected, and the study design.

A. Identify the Research Question’s Goal

  • Estimation: Are we trying to estimate an unknown population parameter (e.g., proportion, mean, difference) with a confidence interval?
  • Hypothesis Testing: Are we testing a specific claim or hypothesis about a population parameter or a relationship between variables using a significance test?

B. Determine the Type of Data and Variables

  • Categorical Data: Deals with counts, proportions, or frequencies. Examples include “proportion of successes,” or “distribution across categories.”
  • Quantitative Data: Deals with measurements or numerical values. Examples include “mean score,” or “average change.”
  • Number of Variables:
    • One Variable: Analyzing a single population parameter (e.g., $ p $ , $ \mu $ ).
    • Two Variables:
      • Comparing two population parameters (e.g., $ p_1 - p_2 $ , $ \mu_1 - \mu_2 $ ).
      • Investigating a relationship between two variables (e.g., Correlation, Linear Regression Models).

C. Identify the Number of Samples/Groups

  • One Sample: Inference about a single population parameter.
  • Two Independent Samples: Comparing two independent groups (e.g., treatment vs. control).
  • Paired Data: Measurements taken from the same subjects under two different conditions or from matched pairs.
  • Multiple Groups/Categories: Comparing distributions or proportions across more than two groups.

D. Key Parameter/Statistic of Interest

  • Proportion ( $ p $ ): For categorical data.
  • Mean ( $ \mu $ ): For quantitative data.
  • Slope ( $ \beta $ ): For the relationship between two quantitative variables in Linear Regression Models.
  • Distribution/Association: For categorical data across multiple groups (e.g., Expected Counts in Two-Way Tables).
