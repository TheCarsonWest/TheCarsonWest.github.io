Topic 11.1: Electric Current

Electric current is the fundamental concept governing the flow of charge in a circuit. It is the bridge between electrostatics (charges at rest) and electrodynamics (charges in motion).

Definition of Current

Electric current ( $ I $ ) is defined as the net rate at which electric charge flows through a cross-sectional area of a conductor. If a net charge $ \Delta q $ passes through an area in a time interval $ \Delta t $ , the average current is defined as:

$$ I = \frac{\Delta q}{\Delta t} $$
The SI unit for current is the ampere (A), where $ 1\text{ A} = 1\text{ C/s} $ .

Instantaneous Current

When the flow of charge is non-uniform, we define the instantaneous current using the derivative:

$$ I = \frac{dq}{dt} $$

Direction and Convention

It is crucial to distinguish between the physical reality of charge carriers and the historical convention:

  • Conventional Current: By convention, the direction of current is defined as the direction in which positive charge would move.
  • Electron Flow: In most metallic conductors, the actual charge carriers are electrons (negative charge). Therefore, the physical electrons move in the opposite direction of the conventional current.

Electric Charge and Electric Force provides the foundational understanding of how these charges interact.

Current Density

While current is a scalar quantity, it is often useful to describe current in terms of a vector field, known as current density ( $ \vec{J} $ ). This describes the current per unit cross-sectional area.

$$ I = \int \vec{J} \cdot d\vec{A} $$
For a uniform current density flowing through a perpendicular area $ A $ :

$$ J = \frac{I}{A} $$
The unit for current density is $ \text{A/m}^2 $ .

Drift Velocity

In a conductor without an electric field, free electrons move randomly due to thermal energy. When an electric field ( $ \vec{E} $ ) is applied, they experience a force and acquire a “drift velocity” ( $ \vec{v}_d $ ), which is a slow, net motion superimposed on their random thermal motion.

If $ n $ is the number density of charge carriers (number of free electrons per unit volume), $ A $ is the cross-sectional area, and $ q $ is the charge of the carrier, the relationship is given by:

$$ I = n|q|Av_d $$
