13.4: Inductance

Inductance is a measure of a conductor’s ability to resist changes in the electric current flowing through it. It is fundamentally related to the magnetic field generated by the current itself. When current in a circuit changes, the resulting change in magnetic flux induces an electromotive force (EMF) that opposes the change, a phenomenon described by Lenz’s Law.

Self-Inductance

Self-inductance, often simply called inductance ( $ L $ ), occurs when a single circuit induces an EMF in itself due to a changing current.

The magnetic flux ( $ \Phi_B $ ) through a coil is proportional to the current ( $ I $ ) flowing through it. We define inductance using the following relationship:

$$ L = \frac{N\Phi_B}{I} $$
Where:

  • $ L $ is the inductance (measured in Henries, H).
  • $ N $ is the number of turns in the coil.
  • $ \Phi_B $ is the magnetic flux through one turn (measured in Webers, Wb).
  • $ I $ is the current (measured in Amperes, A).

Faraday’s Law of Induction states that an induced EMF is generated by a change in magnetic flux. For an inductor, the magnitude of the self-induced EMF ( $ \varepsilon_L $ ) is given by:

$$ \varepsilon_L = -L\frac{dI}{dt} $$
The negative sign indicates that the induced EMF acts to oppose the change in current (Lenz’s Law).

Inductance of a Solenoid

The inductance of a device depends on its geometry and material properties. For a long solenoid with length $ l $ , cross-sectional area $ A $ , and $ N $ total turns, the inductance can be derived from the magnetic field inside the solenoid.

Parameter Value/Relationship
Magnetic Field ( $ B $ ) $ B = \mu_0 n I = \mu_0 \frac{N}{l} I $
Flux per turn ( $ \Phi_B $ ) $ \Phi_B = BA = \mu_0 \frac{N}{l} I A $
Inductance ( $ L $ ) $ L = \frac{N\Phi_B}{I} = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 A}{l} $

As shown, the inductance is purely a geometric property, provided the permeability of the core material remains constant.

Energy in an Inductor

An inductor acts as a storage device for magnetic potential energy. To establish a current $ I $ in an inductor, work must be done against the induced EMF. This work is stored as magnetic field energy.

The power supplied to an inductor is $ P = I|\varepsilon_L| = LI\frac{dI}{dt} $ . Integrating this power over time gives the total energy ( $ U_B $ ) stored:

$$ U_B = \frac{1}{2}LI^2 $$
This energy is stored in the magnetic field within the inductor. This is analogous to the electric potential energy stored in a capacitor, which you can review in Capacitors.

Summary of Relationships

  • Definition: Inductance relates the magnetic flux linkage to the current ( $ N\Phi_B = LI $ ).
  • EMF: A changing current creates an opposing EMF ( $ \varepsilon = -L \frac{dI}{dt} $ ).
  • Energy: Inductors store energy in magnetic fields ( $ U_B = \frac{1}{2}LI^2 $ ).

Understanding inductance is the foundation for analyzing Circuits with Resistors and Inductors (LR Circuits) and Circuits with Capacitors and Inductors (LC Circuits), where the interplay between energy storage elements dictates the behavior of the system.
