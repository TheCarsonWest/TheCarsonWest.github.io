13.5: Circuits with Resistors and Inductors (LR Circuits)

When an inductor and a resistor are connected in series with a DC voltage source, the circuit does not reach its steady-state current instantly. This is due to the property of Inductance, which creates an induced electromotive force (EMF) that opposes changes in current, governed by Lenz’s Law. These circuits are known as LR circuits.

Growth of Current

When a switch is closed, connecting a battery to a series LR circuit, the current ( $ I $ ) does not jump immediately to $ V/R $ . Instead, the inductor creates a back-EMF, $ \epsilon_L = -L\frac{di}{dt} $ , which fights the increase in current. Using Kirchhoffs Loop Rule, the equation for the circuit is:

$$ \epsilon - IR - L\frac{di}{dt} = 0 $$
Solving this differential equation yields the expression for current as a function of time:

$$ I(t) = \frac{\epsilon}{R}(1 - e^{-(R/L)t}) $$

Key Characteristics of Current Growth

  • Initial State ( $ t=0 $ ): $ I = 0 $ . The inductor acts like an open circuit (infinite resistance to change).
  • Steady State ( $ t \rightarrow \infty $ ): $ I = \frac{\epsilon}{R} $ . The inductor acts like a short circuit (ideal wire with no resistance).
  • Time Constant ( $ \tau $ ): Defined as $ \tau = \frac{L}{R} $ . At $ t = \tau $ , the current reaches approximately 63.2% of its maximum value.

Decay of Current

If the battery is removed (or bypassed) while current is flowing, the energy stored in the inductor’s magnetic field is released. The current decays according to the equation:

$$ I(t) = I_0 e^{-(R/L)t} $$
Here, $ I_0 $ is the current present at the instant the battery was disconnected.

Summary of LR Circuit Behavior

The table below summarizes the behavior of the inductor in an LR circuit relative to the time constant $ \tau = L/R $ :

Time Interval Condition Inductor Behavior
** $ t = 0 $ ** Instant switch closed Acts as an open circuit ( $ I=0 $ )
** $ t = \tau $ ** One time constant Current at $ \approx 63% $ of max
** $ t \rightarrow \infty $ ** Steady State Acts as an ideal wire (no voltage drop)

Energy Storage

The inductor stores energy in its magnetic field. The energy ( $ U_L $ ) stored in an inductor carrying current $ I $ is given by:

$$ U_L = \frac{1}{2}LI^2 $$
This energy storage is analogous to the energy stored in a capacitor, as discussed in Resistor Capacitor (RC) Circuits. However, while a capacitor stores energy in an electric field, the inductor stores it in the magnetic field generated by the moving charges.
