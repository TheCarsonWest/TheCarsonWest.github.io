Topic 13.1: Reflection

Reflection is the phenomenon where a wave, such as light or sound, strikes a boundary and “bounces back” into the medium from which it originated. Understanding reflection is foundational for analyzing how optical systems, like mirrors and lenses, form images.

The Law of Reflection

The behavior of light reflecting off a smooth, flat surface is governed by the Law of Reflection. To analyze this, we define a “normal” line, which is an imaginary line perpendicular to the surface at the point of incidence.

The Law of Reflection states that the angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection:

$$ \theta_i = \theta_r $$
Where:

  • $ \theta_i $ is the angle between the incident ray and the normal.
  • $ \theta_r $ is the angle between the reflected ray and the normal.

Both the incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal all lie within the same plane.

Types of Reflection

Reflection is generally categorized based on the nature of the surface the wave strikes.

Type of Reflection Surface Characteristics Resulting Wave Behavior
Specular Reflection Smooth, polished surface (e.g., mirror). Parallel incident rays reflect as parallel rays, preserving the image.
Diffuse Reflection Rough, irregular surface (e.g., paper, wall). Parallel incident rays reflect in various, random directions.

In diffuse reflection, the law of reflection still holds at a microscopic level for every individual ray; however, because the surface is uneven, the normals at different points point in different directions, causing the scattered light. This is why we can see non-shiny objects from any angle, but we see a distinct reflection only in mirrors.

Reflection and Wave Phases

When a wave reflects off a boundary, it may undergo a phase change depending on the relative optical density of the two media.

  • Fixed End (Hard Boundary): When a wave travels from a less dense medium to a more dense medium, the reflected wave undergoes a $ 180^\circ $ ( $ \pi $ radians) phase shift (it inverts).
  • Free End (Soft Boundary): When a wave travels from a more dense medium to a less dense medium, the reflected wave does not undergo a phase shift (it remains upright).

This phase change is critical when analyzing Thin-Film Interference and the behavior of light waves interacting with surfaces.

Applications

Reflection is the primary mechanism behind image formation. When rays from an object are reflected by a surface, they appear to originate from a point behind the mirror. This leads to the study of Images Formed by Mirrors, where we categorize images as real or virtual, and upright or inverted.
