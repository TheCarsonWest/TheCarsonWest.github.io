Conservation of Electric Charge and Charging

The Principle of Conservation of Charge

In physics, electric charge is a fundamental property of matter. The law of Conservation of Electric Charge states that the total electric charge in an isolated system remains constant regardless of the changes that occur within the system. Charge cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred from one body to another or separated within a system.

In any physical process involving charged particles: $$ \sum q_{initial} = \sum q_{final} $$
This principle is a foundational symmetry in electromagnetism and holds true in chemical reactions, nuclear interactions, and macroscopic mechanical processes.

Quantization of Charge

Electric charge is not continuous; it comes in discrete, integer multiples of the fundamental charge $ e $ , which is the charge of a proton. The charge of an electron is $ -e $ .

$$ q = \pm ne $$
Where:

  • $ q $ is the total charge.
  • $ n $ is an integer (number of particles).
  • $ e \approx 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C} $ .

Methods of Charging

Objects can acquire a net charge by transferring electrons. Since protons are locked within the atomic nucleus, charging an object primarily involves the movement of electrons.

Method Mechanism Result
Friction Rubbing two insulators together; electrons transfer due to differences in electron affinity. Both objects gain equal and opposite charges.
Conduction Direct physical contact between a charged object and a neutral conductor. The neutral object gains the same sign of charge as the original charged object.
Induction Bringing a charged object near a conductor without touching, then grounding it. The neutral object gains an opposite sign of charge to the inductor.

Charging by Induction

Induction is a unique method because the original charged object does not lose any of its own charge. Instead, it polarizes the charge carriers in the neutral conductor. When a ground path is provided, electrons either flow into the earth or from the earth to the conductor, leaving the conductor with a net charge opposite to the inducing object.

Conductors and Insulators

The ease with which an object can be charged depends on its material properties. Materials are generally classified based on their ability to allow charge to flow.

  • Conductors: Materials (like metals) with “free electrons” that can move throughout the material. Excess charge distributes itself on the outer surface of a conductor to minimize repulsion.
  • Insulators: Materials (like rubber or glass) where electrons are tightly bound to atoms. Excess charge tends to stay exactly where it is placed.
