From Shakespeare’s Macbeth, select a significant symbol and analyze how it operates within the play and what it reveals about the characters and overall themes.

Symbolism is a recurring motif in William Shakespeare’s Macbeth, where he uses the character’s diction and backgrounds to evoke the personalities and develop the audience’s perspective. The recurring symbol in having and not having children is particularly significant, influencing how certain characters act and what risks they are willing to take. In his play Macbeth, William Shakespeare uses the symbolism of having and not having children to develop the characters’ moralities and to suggest that people with children possess more stability than those without.

In the play, the title character, Macbeth, and his wife Lady Macbeth, are notable for being nobles with no children, which is a trait that sets them apart from all others in the play. Macbeth is a nobleman, but also a warrior, who, as seen in Act I, fights on the front lines rather than simply commanding others. For a nobleman to be fighting in a war is quite risky behavior, which none other in the play take, suggesting that Macbeth is not complacent with his current life like other nobles, and he has nothing to lose in the case of his death, since he has no children. It can be said then, that Shakespeare intended to portray and justify Macbeth’s ambitious and risky behavior by the fact that he has no children. Macbeth lacks the stability given by having children, and thus has a risky and unstable nature, the nature that drives the entire plot of the play.

The symbolism of children as stability is further explored through the King Macbeth murders to ascend, King Duncan. Duncan is a character almost opposite of Macbeth. While Macbeth is young, childless, and a warrior, Dunac is old, has two male heirs, and commands his people to defend his kingdom. While Macbeth does aggressive actions in the first Act, such as “trying to make another Golgatha” in battle, Duncan acts very conservatively, doing things such as promoting his brave warrior Macbeth to satisfy him, and name his son prince of Cumberland to secure his dynasty. While Duncan, who has children, was king, people had feasts, wars were won, and there was political stability. After Duncan was murdered, and under the childless Macbeth’s rule, “orphans and widows are more every day”, nobles, like Banquo, were murdered, and the kingdom was in political disarray. Shakespeare’s contrast between the two men who were king, one with children, and one without, further conveys the idea that children possess stability.

Another character who displays the symbolize the stability of having children is one who loses his, Macduff. Macduff was a thane of Scotland under Duncan who abandoned his castle after Macbeth’s usurpation to retake Scotland with Duncan’s son using the King of England’s army. This, while a bold and unprecedented move to leave your castle vacant, was a calculated maneuver to bring peace to Scotland, suffering under Macbeth. Once again, his conservative and deliberate nature is once again paired with the stability that children represent, until his children are murdered. Upon hearing that his children are dead, he changes dramatically, having a mental breakdown and swearing to kill Macbeth himself, rather than letting his army fight for him. His children gave him something to lose, and a reason not to fight his own battle, but upon their death, he then becomes the only other noble to see battle in the play, connecting the idea that children bring stability, and a lack of them, instability. Shakespeare intentionally writes this chronology to convey his message.

In conclusion, Shakespeare uses the children of characters in Macbeth as a symbol of stability, suggesting that those with children are much more stable than those without. He does this by presenting the tragic hero and main character, Macbeth, as a childless noble with a risk-taking nature, contrasting the rules of two kings, and showing the actions of Macduff before and after the murder of his family.
