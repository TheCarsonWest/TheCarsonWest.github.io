12.3: Magnetic Fields of Current-Carrying Wires and the Biot-Savart Law

Magnetic fields are not just produced by permanent magnets; they are fundamentally created by moving electric charges. When a steady current flows through a wire, it generates a predictable magnetic field in the surrounding space.

The Biot-Savart Law

The Biot-Savart Law provides a general method for calculating the magnetic field $ d\vec{B} $ generated by a small segment of current $ I d\vec{s} $ . It is the magnetic equivalent of Coulomb’s Law for electric fields.

The vector form of the Biot-Savart Law is:

$$ d\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{I d\vec{s} \times \hat{r}}{r^2} $$

Key Components:

  • ** $ d\vec{B} $ **: The infinitesimal magnetic field contribution.
  • ** $ \mu_0 $ **: The permeability of free space, with a value of $ 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \text{ T}\cdot\text{m/A} $ .
  • ** $ I $ **: Current in the wire.
  • ** $ d\vec{s} $ **: Vector representing the length element of the current, pointing in the direction of the current.
  • ** $ \hat{r} $ **: A unit vector pointing from the current element to the point where the field is being calculated.
  • ** $ r $ **: The distance from the source element to the field point.

To find the total magnetic field $ \vec{B} $ , you must perform a vector integration: $ \vec{B} = \int d\vec{B} $ .

Right-Hand Rule

The direction of the magnetic field created by a wire is determined by the Right-Hand Rule for Current-Carrying Wires. If you point your right thumb in the direction of the current, your fingers curl in the direction of the magnetic field lines.

Special Geometries

While the Biot-Savart Law is universal, it is often easier to use Amperes Law for symmetric current distributions. However, Biot-Savart is essential for deriving fields in specific configurations.

Geometry Resulting Magnetic Field Formula Note
Long Straight Wire $$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} $$ Field circles the wire.
Center of Circular Loop $$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2R} $$ Field is perpendicular to the loop plane.
Solenoid (Interior) $$ B = \mu_0 n I $$ $ n = N/L $ (turns per unit length).

Important Observations

  1. Inverse Relationship: For a straight wire, the field magnitude $ B $ is inversely proportional to the distance $ r $ from the wire.
  2. Vector Nature: Always remember that $ \vec{B} $ is a vector field. When solving problems involving multiple wires or complex geometries, you must add the magnetic field vectors using vector addition.
  3. Cross Product: The cross product in the Biot-Savart Law ( $ d\vec{s} \times \hat{r} $ ) implies that the magnetic field is always perpendicular to both the current direction and the position vector. If the point of interest lies along the line of the current, the cross product is zero, meaning the field is zero along the axis of the wire.

For highly symmetric problems, Amperes Law is typically more computationally efficient than direct integration using Biot-Savart.
